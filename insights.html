28 May 2026 Research

Only 4 out of 500 S&P 500 Companies Score 100 on AI Adoption

The AIDE Index — an MIT-linked, objective scoring framework — rated every S&P 500 company on actual AI adoption. The result: 496 companies didn't make 100. Stated AI strategy turned out to be nearly uncorrelated with real implementation. Executives were 3.6× more likely to say "innovation" than "AI" on earnings calls — a telling signal gap. And the top 10 wasn't dominated by tech giants: Walmart, SLB, and AES proved that non-tech companies can lead when they commit.

Why this matters

This is the strongest evidence yet for "Proof Over Promise." If 99.2% of the biggest companies in the world haven't fully adopted AI — and the ones talking about it most aren't the ones doing it most — then the gap between stated ambition and real capability is the defining problem in AI right now. We built our business to close that gap.

11 June 2026 Observation

We Pointed an AI Design Tool at Our Own Site — Here's What Came Back

We gave Claude a brief, then a design tool (Fable 5), then our own site, and asked it to show us directions we hadn't considered. It returned ten distinct concept treatments in a single session — different structures, different moods, different interactive patterns. The useful part wasn't the designs. It was the moment we asked it to tell us what questions we should have asked it in the first place. That reverse-prompt trick changed how we think about working with AI on creative work.

Why this matters

AI is most valuable when it shows you a question you didn't know to ask. The ten concepts aren't the point — they're evidence that an AI design tool, given the right brief, can surface directions a human team would take weeks to reach. We've published them as a separate lab so you can see the range for yourself. This is exploration, not a finished product — but it's the kind of work we do with our own brand before we'd ever do it with yours.
